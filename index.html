Febrile Neutropenia in Adult Oncology
Oncology REF:1538 · v7.0 Current · Review Jul 2027
Give antibiotics BEFORE blood results. Assess, culture & treat within 1 hour of admission or suspicion of fever.
1 · Criteria for treatment — (1) AND (2 or 3)
1 · Neutropenia / suspicionNeutrophils <1.0 × 10⁹/L — or SACT within 4 weeks
2 · FeverTemp >38°C × 1, rigors, or cold/shivery. Temp 37–38°C → repeat after 1hr
3 · Sepsis signsTachycardia, hypotension, tachypnoea, obvious focus
Pure immunotherapy regimens (pembrolizumab, nivolumab etc.) rarely cause neutropenia — use High Risk Red Sepsis pathway instead.
2 · Immediate assessment
1
2 sets blood cultures — peripheral + central line (state line type on form)
2
Check allergy status + previous microbiology for multi-resistant GNB risk
3
Give first dose of antibiotic(s) immediately. Brief history only first — full history after first dose.
BloodsFBC, U&E, LFT, lactate, CRP
CulturesPeripheral + line; MSU; stool if diarrhoea; sputum; swabs if symptomatic
ImagingCXR
Blood cultures before antibiotics where possible — do not delay antibiotics to obtain them.
3 · First-line antibiotic — by allergy status
No penicillin allergy
Pip/Taz 4.5g IV QDS
+ Vancomycin if MRSA/line infection
+ Single dose Gentamicin if high-risk red sepsis or BP unresponsive
Non-severe allergy · Severe immediate-onset · High-dose MTX · Multi-resistant GNB risk
Meropenem 1g IV TDS
+ Vancomycin if MRSA/line
+ Single dose Gentamicin if high-risk
Severe delayed cutaneous (SJS/TEN)
Ciprofloxacin 400mg IV TDS
+ Vancomycin (all patients)
+ Metronidazole 500mg IV TDS if abdo sepsis
+ Gentamicin if high-risk
Vancomycin alternative — teicoplanin
Loading12 mg/kg IV (nearest 200mg) every 12hr × 3 doses
MaintenanceOnce daily — refer to antibiotic website
Red man syndromeNot a contraindication to teicoplanin
Registrar or consultant must review within 24hr (including weekends). Microbiology will ring through positive cultures.
4 · Assessment at 24–48 hours
Not improving
Continue IV ABx · FBC+U&E daily · Repeat cultures if spikes
Improving
Continue IV ABx · FBC+U&E · Consider IV→oral at 24–48hr
No longer neutropenic
Review infective cause · Consider stopping · Discharge if low risk
Stopping criteria — all of:
Apyrexial> 24 hours
HaemodynamicsStable
Ongoing focusNo clinical evidence
Blood culturesNegative after therapy
MASCC score≥21 = low risk
No response >48–72 hours — consider
• Non-infective cause — underlying disease, drug reaction
• Multi-resistant bacteria — review micro, discuss with microbiology
• Line infection — may require line removal
• Invasive fungal infection — if fever/neutropenia >10 days, steroids >3 weeks
• Atypical pathogens — Legionella, PCP, disseminated viral
5 · IV to oral switch — after 24–48hr if low risk, no identified source
High pseudomonas risk / profoundly neutropenicCiprofloxacin 750mg PO BD
Low pseudomonas risk, neutrophils recoveringCo-amoxiclav 625mg PO TDS
Penicillin allergicLevofloxacin 500mg PO OD
Total course 5 days. Source identified → use relevant NUH antibiotic guideline.
Fluoroquinolone cautions
• High C. difficile risk — consult microbiology if known C. diff carrier
• MRSA selection — previous MRSA carriers need microbiology approval
• If on quinolone prophylaxis — discuss with microbiology
• Rare: long-lasting disabling side effects (months to years)
Mouth ulceration
Oral candidiasisFluconazole 100mg PO OD × 14 days
Herpes simplexAciclovir 400mg PO 5× daily × min 5 days
G-CSF — not routinely indicated
Not shown to reduce fever duration or antibiotic use. May be appropriate if ≥2 of: neutrophils <0.1 × 10⁹/L with fever >10 days; uncontrolled primary disease; pneumonia; hypotension; multi-organ dysfunction; invasive fungal infection.
Rectal medications — contraindicated
No rectal examination or medicines (including suppositories/enemas) — risk of bacteraemia.
These regimens rarely cause neutropenia. If a patient is on additional SACT drugs not listed here — follow this guideline.
NUH pure immunotherapy regimens — use High Risk Red Sepsis pathway
AtezolizumabAvelumabCemiplimabDostarlimabDurvalumabIpilimumabIpilimumab + NivolumabNivolumabPembrolizumabTebentefusp

Choose antiemetic regimen based on the highest emetic risk drug in the regimen. Record severity/duration after each cycle using CTCAE grading.

Management of Diarrhoea Post Chemo/Radiotherapy
Oncology REF:1837 · May 2020 Review overdue
Treat aggressively — early intervention prevents life-threatening dehydration and renal failure. Neutropenic patients + diarrhoea = high risk of Gram-negative sepsis → urgent admission.
NCI CTCAE v5 grading — diarrhoea
0
None
1
<4 loose stools/day over baseline · Mild ostomy ↑
2
4–6 stools/day · Moderate ostomy ↑ · Limits instrumental ADL
3
≥7 stools/day · Hospitalisation · Limits self-care ADL
4
Life-threatening · Urgent intervention
SACT type — categorise first
Pure immunotherapyIpilimumab, pembrolizumab, nivolumab etc. → see Immunotherapy tab
Combined immuno + cytotoxicSee immunotherapy tab AND SACT section
Fluoropyrimidines5-FU / Capecitabine → see Fluoropyrimidines tab
TKIs (afatinib, imatinib, sunitinib etc.)Usually mild–moderate. See SACT management + individual prescribing guidelines
Hormonal agents aloneNeutropenic sepsis unlikely — manage as below without step 2a
Initial investigation — non-immunotherapy SACT
Fever ≥38°CPatient may be neutropenic → urgent admission · Follow neutropenic sepsis policy · Stool for C&S + C. diff
Suspected infective diarrhoeaStool for MC&S + C. diff toxin · Maintain hydration (may need admission) · C. diff risk if recent ABx
Management if infective/immunotherapy/fluoropyrimidine causes excluded
1
Review medications contributing to diarrhoea (laxatives, antacids, metoclopramide, domperidone)
2
Exclude overflow diarrhoea from severe constipation
3
Increase clear fluids; low residue diet; avoid dairy
Grade 1 (irinotecan)Loperamide 4mg PO then 2mg every 2hr — continue 12hr after last loose stool
Grade 1 (>12–24hr) or grade 2Loperamide 4mg then 2mg every 2hr (max 16mg/day)
Grade 1–2 not controlled by loperamideAdd codeine phosphate 30mg PO every 6hr, increase to 60mg if needed
Grade 1–2 not responding after 24hrConsider hospital admission for IV hydration
Grade 3–4Hospital admission · IV hydration · Stop ongoing oral/infusional cytotoxics · Loperamide + codeine as above · Consider octreotide
Octreotide: 100–150 micrograms SC TDS or 300–500 micrograms SC over 24hr. Discuss with SpR/consultant.
⚠ Stop anti-diarrhoeals within 12hr of last loose stool — continuing can cause severe ileus.
Cautions in neutropenic patients
No rectal examination or medicines — risk of bacteraemia. No suppositories, enemas, or internal faecal collection systems.
Acute radiation enteritis
Develops during or after week 2 of RT, lasts up to 2 weeks post-completion. More severe with previous abdominal surgery or large bowel volume in field.
Grade 1 (>12–24hr) or grade 2Loperamide 4mg PO then 2mg every 2hr (max 16mg/day)
Grade 1–2 not controlled by loperamideAdd codeine phosphate 30–60mg PO every 6hr
Grade 1–2 not responding after 24hrConsider admission for IV hydration · Discuss stopping RT
Grade 3–4Admit · IV hydration · Stop radiotherapy · Loperamide + codeine · Consider octreotide 150mcg SC TDS
Prostate RT only: consider ispaghula husk sachets (Fybogel) to reduce constipation.
Acute radiation proctitis
Tenesmus, diarrhoea, occasional rectal bleeding. Develops week 2+ of RT.
DietLow residue · Avoid alcohol
Stool softenersIf pain on defecation + no severe diarrhoea
Proctosedyl ointmentConsider — stop enemas
Sucralfate prophylaxisDO NOT USE — ineffective and may worsen proctitis
Late radiation effects — referral required
Chronic radiation enteritisSmall bowel dose ≥45Gy · Abdo pain, perforation, fistula, obstruction, chronic diarrhoea · Refer gastroenterology (radiation enteritis specialist)
Chronic radiation proctitis≥6 months after RT · Tenesmus, diarrhoea, rectal bleeding, stricture · Refer gastroenterology
Fluoropyrimidine diarrhoea can be life-threatening. Grade 3+ requires hospital admission. Check DPYD status before starting treatment.
DPYD testing
DPD deficiency may be cause of fluoropyrimidine-associated diarrhoea. Confirm patient's DPYD status. If unknown, partial, mutant or negative → consider uridine triacetate if criteria met. See SOP for DPD testing at NUH.
Grade 2 (4–6 stools/day) — able to maintain oral fluids
1
Review history (medication, dietary intake) — may include telephone review
2
Assess for C. diff (if recent ABx or positive history) — send stool if indicated
3
Review loperamide compliance; reinforce maximal doses if not taken
4
Capecitabine patients — discuss with oncology SpR/consultant re: stop or continue
5
Bloods: FBC, U&E, LFT, Bone, Mg, CRP; stool for C. diff toxin + MC&S
6
If stable → discharge with strict return instructions if worsening
Grade 3+ (≥7 stools/day) or grade 2 in frail/unable to maintain fluids — admit
1
Stop capecitabine — store patient's supply securely for potential later use
2
Cannulate · Bloods: FBC, U&E, LFT, Bone, Mg, CRP, clotting + glucose/TFTs as indicated
3
Stool: C. diff toxin + MC&S
4
IV fluids — adjust to keep K⁺ >4 mmol/L · ABG if metabolic acidosis suspected
5
Nil by mouth until symptoms settle (sips to swallow medication permitted)
6
Strict fluid balance · Stool chart · Daily weights
7
Regular loperamide 2–4mg PO 4–8× day; if no improvement >24hr or >Grade 2 → add codeine 60mg PO QDS. SC codeine if oral not tolerated.
8
Prokinetic antiemetics (metoclopramide, domperidone) — avoid where possible. Prescribe parenterally where possible.
9
Octreotide 300mcg SC over 24hr if not improving — discuss with SpR/consultant. Review daily up to max 1500mcg/24hr.
10
Daily bloods (FBC, U&E, LFT, Bone, Mg) for 72hr. Consider CXR/AXR if pain/distension. Reduce cytotoxic dose next cycle.
Diarrhoea on checkpoint inhibitors may indicate immune-mediated colitis. Can occur at any time including months after last dose.
Immunotherapy-associated diarrhoea — key principles
1
All cases must be discussed with a senior oncology clinician
2
Send stool for enteropathogens + C. diff toxin (exclude infection)
3
Close monitoring and early intervention are essential to prevent progression
4
Preventative budesonide — no benefit
5
Combination therapy causes earlier and more frequent symptoms than single agents
Checkpoint inhibitor agents
IpilimumabPembrolizumabNivolumabDurvalumabAvelumabAtezolizumabCemiplimab
Management per Clinical Guidelines for Immunotherapy (ICPI) Related Toxicity for Solid Tumours — available on NUH intranet.
Chemical Pleurodesis
Respiratory Nursing & Clinical Procedure Guide Current
⚠ A BUBBLING CHEST DRAIN MUST NEVER BE CLAMPED — risk of fatal tension pneumothorax.
At-a-glance key facts
Analgesia pre-procedure5–10mg oramorph, 1 hour before. Avoid NSAIDs where possible.
Lidocaine dose>75kg → 25ml · <75kg → 20ml (1% solution, 3mg/kg, max 250mg)
Talc dose4–5g sterile graded talc in 50ml 0.9% saline. Prepared by medic immediately prior.
Talc stabilityStable for 1 hour only once in solution
Post-LA wait10 minutes (elevate drain / gravity — no clamping needed)
Best practiceAfter instillation, keep pleura in contact with agent. Document method in notes.
Why this matters
• Pleurodesis creates a painful chemical pleurisy — adequate analgesia is essential (Roberts et al 2010)
• NSAIDs may reduce the inflammatory response required for pleurodesis success (Antunes et al 2003)
• Gravity avoids the need for drain clamping — which is dangerous if the drain is bubbling
• Talc must be prepared immediately prior — do not leave unattended to minimise wrong-route risk
Equipment checklist
💊 Prescribed analgesia
💊 Prescribed premedication
🧪 Talc 4g with adaptor for syringe
💉 1% Lidocaine solution (3mg/kg; max 25ml)
🔬 2 × 50ml syringes
🧴 Chloraprep® wands (70% IPA + 2% chlorhexidine)
🔩 2 × needles for drawing up
💧 100ml 0.9% saline
🧤 Sterile dressing pack + sterile gloves
🔒 Chest drain clamps (if available)
On base wards talc is dispensed on a named-patient basis. Ensure talc and analgesia are available before starting.

⚠ CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING
A BUBBLING CHEST DRAIN MUST NEVER BE CLAMPED.
Doing so risks causing a potentially fatal tension pneumothorax.
Key safety points
Bubbling drainNEVER clamp — tension pneumothorax risk
NSAIDsAvoid where possible — may reduce inflammatory response required for success
Talc stabilityStable 1hr only once in solution — prepare immediately prior, do not leave unattended
Syringe labellingLabel clearly immediately on drawing up — LA and talc syringes must be distinguished
Wrong-route preventionMedic takes syringe directly to bedside. Do not leave unattended at any stage.
ConsentValid informed consent by medic with procedure knowledge — documented in notes
